The Challenge

This firm had built a genuinely exceptional reputation over two decades. Their Education Hub offered Australia's only ASIC-approved, internationally recognised share market diploma. Their Investment Hub managed client portfolios with 80+ years of combined industry experience across the team. They held FPA and CPA Australia CPD recognition. By every measure of substance, they were the clear market leader.

But substance alone does not guarantee visibility. Global platforms were encroaching. Coursera was pushing aggressively into the Australian financial education space with enormous content budgets. IG, a globally recognised finance brand, was expanding its educational content to capture investment-intent searches. Both were targeting the exact keywords this firm had built its organic presence around.

The deeper problem was structural. The firm operated two distinct hubs -- Education and Investment -- that served different audiences, answered different questions, and needed to rank for different keyword clusters. Search engines were not clearly distinguishing between the two. This created entity confusion: the brand was strong, but the signal architecture was blurred. When Google and AI systems could not clearly understand which hub answered which query, visibility fragmented.

The brief was clear: defend market leadership, clarify the dual-entity architecture, and make the brand the unambiguous recommended choice in both spaces -- not just for today's search algorithms, but for the AI systems increasingly influencing discovery.

The Reviewly Approach

Reviewly applied Entity-first SEO and the HEAT Framework across both hubs, treating them as two distinct but related entities within a single brand architecture. The goal was not to rank for more keywords -- it was to make each hub so clearly understood by search engines and AI systems that recommendation became automatic.

1

Entity Architecture Audit

Mapped the full entity landscape for both hubs. Identified where Google was conflating Education and Investment signals, and where competitor entities were encroaching on branded search territory.

2

Dual-Hub Entity Separation

Built distinct entity signals for each hub: separate schema architecture, separate topical authority clusters, separate review and credential signals. Each hub became independently recognisable to search and AI systems.

3

HEAT Framework Content Architecture

Applied the HEAT framework (High-trust, Entity-aligned, Authoritative, Topical) to content across both hubs. Every piece of content was built to compound authority rather than chase individual rankings.

4

Competitor Defence Signals

Built explicit competitive positioning signals that allowed search engines to understand why this firm -- not Coursera, not IG -- was the authoritative Australian source. ASIC approval, CPD recognition, and 742 verified reviews became structured data, not just page copy.

5

AI Recommendation Optimisation

Structured the brand's entity data so AI systems could confidently recommend the right hub for the right query. This included Wikidata alignment, structured FAQ schema, and review signal amplification.

Why This Matters for AI Search

The same entity clarity that drives Google rankings is what makes a brand recommendable by AI systems. When ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google AI Mode is asked about financial education or investment management in Australia, they draw on structured signals: verified credentials, review data, schema markup, and topical authority clusters. A brand with blurred entity signals gets ignored. A brand with clear entity architecture gets recommended.

For this firm, the dual-hub architecture meant AI systems could now confidently recommend the Education Hub for diploma and course queries, and the Investment Hub for IMA and portfolio queries -- independently, accurately, and without confusion. Global competitors with larger budgets cannot replicate the entity trust built through verified credentials, 742 reviews, and 20 years of structured authority signals.

This is what being Worth Choosing looks like in practice. Not a ranking. Not a keyword. A brand that is clearly understood, distinctly positioned, consistently verified, and confidently recommended -- even when global giants try to take their market.

Frequently Asked Questions

What was the main challenge this firm faced?

Intensifying competition from global platforms (Coursera in education, IG in investments) encroaching on their core Australian market. They needed to defend their leadership position while maintaining clarity between their Education Hub and Investment Hub offerings.

How is this different from traditional SEO?

Traditional SEO optimises pages for keyword rankings. Entity-first SEO structures brands for topical authority and recommendation. For this firm, that meant building precise entity architecture that allowed search engines and AI systems to distinguish, trust, and recommend each hub independently.

What does 83% Share of Voice mean in practice?

It means that across the keyword landscape where this firm competes, they capture 83% of the visible search real estate. In a market with global competitors like Coursera and IG, this represents dominant visibility and trust that cannot be bought with content volume alone.
